\section{Medical Comparison}
\section{Medical Comparison: Cortisol vs. Cortisone}

\subsection{Overview}
Cortisol and Cortisone are closely related corticosteroids, but they differ in biological activity and potency.

\subsection{Biological Activity}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cortisol (Hydrocortisone)}: The biologically active form of the hormone. It can directly bind to glucocorticoid receptors to exert its effects.

  \item \textbf{Cortisone}: A prodrug (biologically inactive). It must be converted into Cortisol to become active.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Activation Pathway}
The conversion of Cortisone to Cortisol is mediated by the enzyme \textbf{11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1 (11β-HSD1)}, which is primarily located in the liver but also found in other tissues like adipose tissue and the brain.

\subsection{Relative Potency}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cortisol}: Relative Potency = 1 (Reference standard).

  \item \textbf{Cortisone}: Relative Potency ≈ 0.8. Cortisone is generally considered slightly less potent than Cortisol.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Therapeutic Use Cases}
\subsubsection{Cortisol (Hydrocortisone)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Adrenal Insufficiency}: Primary treatment for Addison's disease.

  \item \textbf{Acute Allergic Reactions}: Used for rapid effect in severe allergies.

  \item \textbf{Topical Applications}: Common in creams for skin inflammation and itching.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Cortisone}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Joint and Tendon Inflammation}: Often administered via local injection (e.g., for bursitis or arthritis).

  \item \textbf{Systemic Inflammation}: Used orally for various autoimmune and inflammatory conditions where a prodrug approach is acceptable.
\end{itemize}
